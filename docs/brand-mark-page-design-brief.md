# Brand Mark Page: Design & Copy Brief

**Page:** `/work/brand-mark`
**Business:** Untitled Mixed Media LLC — Spencer Bennett, Richmond, VA
**Date:** April 2026
**Purpose of this document:** Comprehensive reference for web design review. Explains the offer, the customer, the page architecture, every section's copy and design rationale, and how the two work together. Intended for a designer coming to this fresh.

---

## 1. The Offer

**What it is:** A hand-painted, hand-lettered original on a sealed wood panel, made specifically for one business, hung on their wall within the same week. Price: $500. Payment split: $250 deposit to start, $250 balance due only after the piece is installed and the client accepts it.

**What "the words" means:** The client chooses the words — their logo, a neighborhood name, their family name, a motto, a phrase their customers already associate with the place. Spencer handles the lettering, the layout, the wood panel construction, the paint, the weathering finish, and the professional installation. The client provides words and a direction. Spencer delivers a finished piece.

**Physical specs:** Wood panel, up to 16 square feet, 2–3 color palette matched to the client's brand, finished anywhere on a spectrum from sharp-modern to heavily-aged ghost-sign. The Surface Shield protective topcoat is included. Hardware and professional hanging are included. A Forever Kit (the exact paint + a detail brush) is included for touch-ups.

**The Collector's Guarantee:** The client pays $250 upfront. When the piece is hung in their space and they see it in context, they decide. They accept it and pay the remaining $250. Or they decline it and Spencer takes it home. The client owes nothing more if they decline. This is the primary risk reversal on the page: the client never pays the full amount before seeing the finished piece in their actual space.

**The mural credit:** If the client ever commissions a full mural from Untitled Mixed Media, their $500 comes back as a full credit, dollar for dollar, with no expiration and no conditions. This means the Brand Mark effectively costs nothing if the relationship grows into a mural. This mechanism serves two purposes: it reduces the perceived cost at $500, and it seeds the pipeline for larger future commissions.

**The scarcity mechanism:** The launch cohort is 9 Brand Marks at $500, one per Richmond neighborhood. When a neighborhood fills, the launch price is gone. After round one, the price steps to $750, then $1,000. The limit is real, not manufactured: Spencer can paint approximately one Brand Mark per week while still keeping capacity for larger commissions.

**Conversion destination:** All CTAs link to `/contact?type=brand-mark` — a contact form pre-filtered to the Brand Mark inquiry type. The form asks 4 project questions plus contact info. Total time: under 2 minutes.

## 2. The Customer

**Who they are:** Small business owners in Richmond, Virginia. Restaurants, coffee shops, breweries, barbershops, boutiques, gyms, wellness studios, service businesses. They've invested real thought and money into what their place looks like on the inside — the furniture, the menu design, the overall feel. But their signage and identity materials are generic. Not bad. Just not theirs.

**Their internal state:** They want their space to feel established. Permanent. Like it belongs in the neighborhood, not like it could have been anywhere. They're aware of the gap between the quality of their work and what their space communicates from the street or at a glance inside. But they haven't found the right solution. Custom sign shops are expensive ($2,000+) and slow. Vinyl prints are cheap but look it. A full mural is more than they need right now and requires wall prep, landlord approval, and weeks of planning.

**Their hesitations:**
- "I don't know what words to put on it."
- "My landlord won't let me paint."
- "I don't have a logo file."
- "I've never hired an artist."
- "I might move."
- "Is $500 a lot for a sign?"

All six objections are handled explicitly on the page with direct, brief answers.

**What they want to feel:** That their space communicates craft and intention. That it looks like a real person runs it. That when someone walks in for the first time, they feel the place has been here, that it has a history, that it was built by someone who cares.

## 3. The Messaging Framework

This page uses the StoryBrand structure: the customer is the hero, Spencer is the guide, and there is a specific villain.

**The villain:** The catalog sign. The disposable, template-printed identity that communicates "I ordered this from a default menu." It's not that the client has something ugly — it's that what they have is generic. It could belong to any business on any block. It signals temporary. It signals "I didn't choose this, I just bought it." The page names this problem directly in Section 3 and frames the Brand Mark as the solution.

**The hero:** The business owner who already does good work inside and wants the outside — or the interior identity — to reflect that quality.

**The guide:** Spencer Bennett. He's introduced briefly in Section 4 (How It Works), not as a credentials showcase but as someone who has built the right process: "If you want something hand-painted but aren't ready for a full mural, this is where to start." The guide positioning is restrained. The page stays customer-focused throughout.

**The controlling idea for Brand Mark:** "Your brand deserves the wall, not just the window." The page doesn't state this verbatim, but it's the logic underneath everything.

**Messaging rules this page follows:**
- Lead with the customer's problem, not Spencer's credentials.
- Customer is always the hero. Spencer is always the guide.
- No em dashes in copy (replaced with periods, commas, colons, or parentheses).
- Never use "signage" when "sign" or "original" is more precise.
- The word "original" is used deliberately and often. It's the distinguishing fact.

## 4. Page Architecture: The Funnel Logic

The page is structured as a long-form offer page in ten sections. The sections follow a deliberate psychological sequence: create desire, frame the problem, present the offer, prove it, handle objections, create urgency, close.

```
01  Hero                  — Make the core promise. Show the thing. Who this is for.
02  Proof Bar             — Establish the key facts before anything else.
03  Choose the Words      — Kill the "I don't know what to put on it" friction early.
04  The Problem           — Name the villain. Frame the gap. Show what it costs.
05  How It Works          — The 3-step process. Reduce uncertainty. Introduce Spencer.
06  The Guarantee         — Remove risk entirely. The Collector's Guarantee.
07  Value Stack           — What $500 gets you. The total value is $1,500+.
08  This Still Works If   — Handle every remaining objection.
09  First Come, First     — Real scarcity. 9 neighborhoods. Prices step up.
       Painted
10  FAQ                   — Specific questions answered. Final remaining friction.
11  Closing CTA           — Restate the offer. Emotional close. Final CTA.
```

**Why the order matters:**

Section 3 (Choose the Words) appears before the problem section. This is intentional: the single biggest hidden friction for this offer is "I don't know what I'd put on it." If a visitor reaches the problem section without understanding what the product actually is, they disengage before they feel the need. Resolving the "but what would I even say?" question first keeps them reading into the problem.

Section 5 (The Guarantee) appears before the value stack, not after. Standard offer-page structure puts the guarantee after the price reveal. Here it's reversed deliberately: the guarantee reduces the perceived risk of the whole offer, and reading it before the price makes $500 feel smaller. The visitor enters the pricing section already knowing they can decline at install time.

Multiple CTAs appear throughout: end of Section 5 (How It Works), inside the pricing card in Section 7 (Value Stack), end of Section 9 (Scarcity), and the final closing. The copy at each CTA is identical ("Claim Your Spot") to train a single response rather than introducing choice at each moment.

## 5. The Design System

Understanding the visual language requires understanding the five-font hierarchy and the color system. Both are fixed brand assets.

### Typography

| Role | Font | Used for |
|------|------|----------|
| Display / headings | Canela (Regular, Italic, Medium) | Section headlines, pull quotes, the big typographic specimens |
| Body text | Lyon Text | All paragraph copy, descriptions, FAQ answers |
| UI / navigation / buttons | Gotham | CTA buttons, labels, uppercase tracking text |
| Tags / metadata | Berkeley Mono | Section labels ("How It Works"), prices, status tags, captions |
| Logo only | Canela Blackletter | Site nav logo; not used on this page |

Canela is a contemporary serif with editorial weight — it reads as expensive and considered, which is appropriate for an offer positioned as "fine art for your brand identity." Lyon Text is a reading font with high legibility at body sizes. Gotham is the structural workhorse: clean, authoritative, uppercase. Berkeley Mono signals technical precision and catalog-style metadata.

The type scale is fluid (using `clamp()`) across 9 levels from `--text-fluid-xs` (12–13px) to `--text-fluid-5xl` (44–72px). The hero headline uses `--text-fluid-5xl`. Section headings typically use `--text-fluid-3xl` or `--text-fluid-4xl`. Body copy uses `--text-fluid-base` (17–18px). Labels and metadata use `--text-fluid-xs` in Gotham uppercase with wide tracking.

### Color

| Name | Hex | Role |
|------|-----|------|
| Bone black | `#000205` | Page background (true near-black, not pure black) |
| Titanium white | `#EEECE9` | Primary text, active elements, hero type, CTAs |
| Soft gray | `#B3B2B0` | Body copy, descriptions — readable on dark bg |
| Smoke gray | `#767676` | Secondary labels, metadata, captions |
| Slate gray | `#3C3D3E` | Borders, dividers, structural lines |
| Midnight graphite | `#1E1F22` | Alternate section background (How It Works, Value Stack, Scarcity) |

**Contrast note:** Smoke gray on midnight graphite fails WCAG AA for normal text (3.62:1). On this page, smoke gray on midnight graphite is used only for decorative elements (numbers, captions), never for substantive copy. Body copy in midnight graphite sections uses soft gray instead.

### Spacing and Layout

The page uses `max-w-7xl mx-auto` (1280px) as the outer container for all sections. Inside each section, reading columns are constrained to `max-w-2xl` (~42rem, approximately 65 characters per line — the optimal reading measure). Headlines, images, and grid layouts are allowed to use the full 7xl width. This produces the editorial sense of "breathing room": the layout is wide, but text is never stretched uncomfortably.

Section vertical gaps: `mb-32 lg:mb-40` (128px / 160px). This is the primary change that produces breathing room — the previous design used 96px gaps, which made sections feel stacked against each other. The increase to 160px on desktop is the difference between "a stack of cards" and "a designed page."

## 6. Section-by-Section: Copy and Design

### Section 1: Hero

**Design intent:** Editorial split grid. Typography as protagonist left, product image right. The page declares what it is in the first 10 seconds without explanation or buildup.

**Layout:** `lg:grid-cols-12`. Left column is 7/12. Right column is 5/12. On mobile, stacks vertically — text first, image second. The image uses a `4/5` portrait aspect ratio, which gives it height without taking the full width. A small `figcaption` floats at the bottom of the image with "Plate I" and "Hand-lettered, sealed wood" — catalog notation that signals the piece is a collectible, not a product.

**Copy:**

> *The Brand Mark — No. 01 / Richmond, VA*
>
> **Your words,**
> **hand-painted**
> **on wood.**
>
> Ships in 5 business days · Price $500 · Deposit $250
>
> A hand-painted original for Richmond businesses that want their space to look established, permanent, and worth trusting, without mural pricing, landlord approval, wall prep, or weeks of waiting.
>
> [Claim Your Spot] [See How It Works ↓]
>
> *4 questions. Under 2 minutes.*

**What the copy is doing:** The headline is three lines of direct declaration. No adjectives, no claims — just the product. The inline fact row (ships in / price / deposit) eliminates the three most common preliminary questions before the visitor has to scroll. The paragraph frames the benefit as a list of things this product doesn't require — removing objections before they're raised. "Without mural pricing, landlord approval, wall prep, or weeks of waiting" speaks directly to the alternative paths the customer has already considered and rejected.

**What the design is doing:** The 7/5 grid split gives the text more room than the image — the image is present, but the hierarchy is clear. The image's `4/5` portrait ratio means it extends below the fold on most screens, creating a visual hook to scroll. The "Plate I / Hand-lettered, sealed wood" caption treats the product as a museum piece before the page has made the argument for why it should be treated that way.

**Layout:** `grid-cols-2 md:grid-cols-4`. Full `max-w-7xl`. Each stat is a heading + one-line description. No borders around individual stats — the grid provides structure without boxing.

The "02 — This one" label with white borders elevates the center option into a featured position.

**What the copy is doing:** The guarantee headline is a single declarative sentence that removes all residual risk in 12 words. The paragraph restates the mechanics precisely so there's no ambiguity. **What the design is doing:** Space is the design here. The guarantee is given more vertical room and a larger heading than any section except the hero. The "Collector's Guarantee" label in Mono uppercase names the guarantee like a product — which makes it feel like a deliberate commitment rather than standard fine print. Left-alignment on a page that otherwise uses centered or mixed alignment gives this section a different register: it reads as a personal statement, not a pitch.

### Section 8: This Still Works If (Objections)

**Design intent:** Six objection-handling cards in a `md:grid-cols-2 lg:grid-cols-3` grid. Each card has a `border-t border-slate-gray` top separator and a conditional-phrased heading ("Your walls are in rough shape" rather than "What if my walls are in rough shape?"). The conditional framing treats each objection as a statement of fact rather than a question — acknowledging the situation without melodrama.

**Layout:** `max-w-7xl`. Section headline left-aligned `max-w-2xl`. 3-column grid on desktop, 2-column on tablet, single column on mobile. Each card: `pt-6 border-t border-slate-gray`.

**Copy:**

> **This still works if…**

> **Your walls are in rough shape.**
> The panel hangs on any wall surface. I mount it with hardware matched to your wall type. Rough surfaces aren't a problem.

> **Your landlord won't let you paint.**
> The panel hangs like a framed piece and comes down without a mark. No alterations, no lease violations, no permission needed.

> **You don't have a logo or a file to send.**
> Tell me the words. I handle the lettering, the layout, and the finish. An idea is enough to start.

> **You've never hired an artist before.**
> You tell me what you want. I guide the rest. One conversation, same week, done.

> **You're not sure you'll stay in this space.**
> The panel travels with you. Your first location, your second location, a pop-up, a farmers market booth.

> **You're not sure what finish you want.**
> I'll show you the range, and we'll pick the right one in 15 minutes.

**What the copy is doing:** The "This still works if…" framing works because it treats the visitor's situation with respect. It doesn't say "Don't worry about your rough walls!" — it says "Your walls are in rough shape. The panel hangs on any wall surface." Short, declarative, done. Each answer is 1–3 sentences. The section's entire job is to clear remaining friction, and brevity is the right tool for that.

**What the design is doing:** Three columns on desktop puts all six objections visible without scrolling (on most viewport heights). The border-top separator on each card creates visual structure without boxes — the grid cards breathe. The 3-column layout is the tightest grid on the page, which reads appropriately as a list to scan rather than content to read slowly.

Neighborhood list: `divide-y divide-slate-gray`, each row `grid-cols-12`, with number / name / status. ## 7. Conversion Mechanisms

Three conversion systems run on the page in addition to inline CTAs.

**Sticky Mobile CTA:** Fixed at the bottom of the viewport on mobile (`md:hidden`). Slides up when the hero scrolls out of view using IntersectionObserver, slides back down when the hero is visible. Shows "The Brand Mark / $500 / Same week" with a "Claim Your Spot" button. Uses `env(safe-area-inset-bottom)` for iOS home bar clearance. Min tap target 44px on the button. This ensures the primary CTA is always accessible on mobile without the visitor having to scroll back to the hero.

**Exit Intent Popup:** Fires on desktop when the cursor leaves the viewport toward the top (exit intent), and on mobile when the visitor scrolls up significantly (indicating intent to leave). Copy:

> *Before you go.*
>
> The Brand Mark is hand-painted, lasts 20+ years, and the full $500 rolls into a future mural. 4 quick questions to hold your spot.
>
> [Claim Your Spot] / I'll keep the sticker.

The decline label ("I'll keep the sticker") is the cost-of-inaction statement: the visitor actively chooses a sticker over an original. Accessibility: focus trap, Escape key handler, `role="dialog"`, `aria-modal="true"`, focus return on close.

**CTA consistency:** Every CTA on the page says "Claim Your Spot." Not "Contact Us," not "Get a Quote," not "Learn More." One action, one label, repeated consistently. The label is active and specific — "claim" implies a limited supply, "spot" implies a reserved position.

## 8. Mobile vs Desktop Strategy

**On iPhone (the primary design target):**
The iPhone experience is the baseline. Every section stacks vertically in a clean single column. The sticky CTA keeps conversion accessible at all times. Images stack below their associated text. Section spacing is generous but not wasteful. Font sizes never drop below 16px (prevents iOS Safari auto-zoom on form inputs). The FAQ accordion keeps the page short. The sticky CTA and exit popup provide two rescue mechanisms if the visitor starts to leave.

**On Desktop (the artistic statement):**
Desktop is additive — everything that works on iPhone works on desktop, plus editorial complexity. Twelve-column grid splits appear throughout: hero (7/5), How It Works steps (7/5 per step), Scarcity (4/8), FAQ (3/9 sticky label), Closing (7/5). These are not present on mobile. The typography scales to its largest sizes. The full-bleed image break in Section 4 is more dramatic at desktop width. The neighborhood list at `text-fluid-2xl` Canela reads at approximately 36px on desktop — that's broadsheet-headline scale. The design earns the "editorial" label on desktop because every layout decision makes the content feel designed for the medium, not just reflowed from mobile.

## 9. What a Design Review Should Evaluate

A web designer reviewing this page should assess:

1. **Does the visual hierarchy support the persuasive hierarchy?** The most important content (hero headline, guarantee, closing CTA) should have the most visual weight. If any of these sections feel visually equal to supporting content, the hierarchy needs adjustment.

2. **Do the images do conceptual work?** Every image on the page is placed because it answers an implicit question at that moment in the funnel. If an image is decorative rather than explanatory, it's not earning its space.

3. **Does the typography specimen section (Section 3) demonstrate the product?** This is the most important single section on the page for overcoming the "I don't know what to put on it" objection. If the type feels generic or the scale is too small, the section fails.

4. **Does the guarantee section have enough room?** This is the primary risk-reversal on the page. It should feel like a statement from the designer, not a fine-print clause. If it feels small, it needs more space and weight.

5. **Is the scarcity section's neighborhood list readable at a glance?** A Richmond business owner should be able to find their neighborhood instantly. The `text-fluid-2xl` scale is intentional — the neighborhood names need to be large enough to scan, not read line by line.

6. **Does the pricing card image justify the price?** The `trolley-barn/finished-texture.jpg` beside the $500 pricing card needs to show craft clearly enough that the price feels obvious, not steep. If the image is ambiguous or low-detail, it should be swapped for a higher-contrast close-up.

7. **Does the closing CTA section feel like a conclusion, not a repeat?** The closing is doing emotional work, not informational work. The left-aligned copy, the portrait image, and the specific scene ("someone stops, someone takes a photo") should feel like a final statement, not a fourth CTA block.
