## The Dual Goal of Every Post

Every journal post serves two audiences simultaneously:

1. **Google and AI chatbots** — so UMM gets found when someone types "hand-painted signs Richmond VA" into Google, or asks ChatGPT "how much does a mural cost in Richmond"
2. **Real humans who are about to hire someone** — so that when a prospect finds the post, it builds enough trust and credibility that they contact Spencer

These goals are not in conflict. Content that actually answers a real question, with specific numbers and genuine expertise, is exactly what both Google and AI systems want to cite.

**The test for any post:** If a business owner in Richmond googled this question and found this page, would they feel like they got a real, complete, useful answer from someone who clearly knows what they're talking about? If yes, publish it. If it feels like filler or content marketing, fix it.

## How AI Chatbots Work (Why This Matters)

When someone asks ChatGPT "how much does a mural cost in Richmond VA" or asks Perplexity "best muralist in Richmond Virginia," these systems search the web, read pages, and pull specific facts into their answer. They cite sources with links.

To get cited:

- **Answer the question directly in the first paragraph.** AI systems extract answers from the first 100 words of a post. If the answer is buried, it doesn't get extracted.
- **Use specific numbers and ranges.** "Between $18 and $110 per square foot" gets cited. "Varies depending on complexity" does not.
- **Use tables for comparisons.** Comparison tables (vinyl vs. hand-painted, interior vs. exterior, simple vs. complex) are extracted and reproduced by AI systems almost verbatim.
- **Write FAQ sections.** Questions formatted as H3 headers followed by direct 40-60 word answers are a primary citation source for AI chatbots.
- **Include dates.** Dated content is weighted higher by AI systems because they prioritize recent information.
- **Name the author with credentials.** "Spencer Bennett, muralist and sign painter in Richmond, Virginia" establishes the E-E-A-T (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness) signals that AI systems use to decide if content is worth citing.

**What actively hurts AI citation:** Keyword stuffing. Writing that sounds like it was written for a search engine rather than a human reader. AI systems are trained to recognize and deprioritize it.

## Where It Can Go Broader

Spencer said the journal should feel like a brain dump: thoughts, ideas, all kinds of stuff. That is correct. Not every post needs to be a FAQ. These types of posts also belong here:

**Opinion posts:** What Spencer actually thinks about something in the industry, in Richmond, in the craft. "Why most public art fails" or "The problem with mural festivals" or "Why I stopped accepting certain projects." These get shared, get linked, and build brand character.

**Field observations:** Things noticed while working. Details that only someone doing this work would see. These build E-E-A-T more than any technically-optimized post because they are genuinely irreplaceable.

**Richmond-specific essays:** The city's relationship with its built environment, its history of demolition and preservation, the neighborhoods changing fastest. This is topical authority. It also makes UMM sound like a Richmond institution rather than a vendor.

**Honest takes on adjacent topics:** VARA rights for muralists (we have a draft on this), public art funding, working with property owners who don't understand the process. These address real questions real clients have.

The rule for all of them: it has to be real. Specific. Something only Spencer could write. The AI writing detection section in the seo-audit skill reference has a list of patterns to avoid — vague observations, overused phrases, em dashes. Concrete detail is the opposite of those patterns.

## Voice and Tone Rules

These rules apply to every post, every time. No exceptions.

**No em dashes.** Use a period, comma, colon, semicolon, or parentheses instead. This is a brand rule, not a style preference.

**The customer is the hero.** Spencer/UMM is the guide. Posts should address what the customer is thinking, worrying about, or trying to decide. Not what Spencer finds interesting about his own craft (unless the post is explicitly a field observation or opinion piece).

**Specific over vague.** "$18-28 per square foot" not "affordable." "Virginia humidity averages 70 percent relative humidity" not "the climate is hard on signs."

**Show the craft, name the technique.** Readers trust specific. "We use oil gilding size with 23.5k gold leaf" builds more trust than "we use the best materials." Name things.

**Short paragraphs.** One idea each. If a paragraph runs longer than 4 sentences, it probably contains two ideas. Split it.

**Direct sentences.** Lead with the point. Do not bury the answer at the end of a long setup.

**First-person is allowed.** Spencer has opinions. He should share them. "My own position as someone who paints signs for a living" is more credible than pretending to be a neutral authority.

**Do not use:** synergy, leverage, seamless, journey, transformation, innovative, solution (without the specific problem), curated, elevate, passionate about.

**Do not write:** "Great question." "Certainly." "I'd be happy to help." "In conclusion." These patterns mark content as AI-generated and undermine credibility.

## SEO Mechanics Checklist

Every post should hit these before publishing:

- [ ] Title contains the primary search query (or a close natural variant)
- [ ] Title is 55-60 characters (fits in Google SERPs without truncation)
- [ ] Meta description contains the primary keyword and a reason to click
- [ ] Meta description is 150-160 characters
- [ ] H1 matches or closely reflects the title
- [ ] Primary keyword appears in the first 100 words
- [ ] At least one internal link to the relevant service page (murals, signs, brand mark, contact)
- [ ] At least one comparison table or data table if the topic supports it
- [ ] FAQ section with 3-5 questions formatted as bold questions followed by paragraph answers
- [ ] "Richmond, Virginia" or "Richmond, VA" appears naturally 2-4 times throughout
- [ ] Author is set to "Spencer Bennett" in frontmatter
- [ ] Date is current (not future-dated)
- [ ] `featured: true` for cornerstone posts in each pillar (one per pillar maximum)

## For Spencer's Copywriting Expert

If you are the expert writer hired to produce or improve UMM journal content, the important things to know:

**What UMM is:** A one-person hand-painting studio in Richmond, Virginia. Spencer Bennett paints murals, signs, and restorations. Everything is hand-painted. No vinyl, no digital printing.

**What we are trying to accomplish with the journal:** Dominate AI chatbot results and Google search for every query related to murals, signs, and public art in Richmond, Virginia. Every potential client in Richmond who types a question into Google or an AI chatbot about any of these topics should find UMM.

**What we are NOT doing:** Writing content marketing. We are writing genuinely useful posts that happen to also rank well. If a post does not actually answer a real question a real person would have, it is not worth publishing.

**The voice:** Direct. Specific. Expert. First-person is fine. Opinions are welcome. The ghost signs post and the cost breakdown post are the best examples of the voice at its best. Read those before writing anything new.

**What will get posts rejected:**
- Em dashes (replace with periods, commas, colons, or semicolons)
- Vague language where specific language is possible ("high quality" instead of "$18 per square foot")
- The customer framed as a passive beneficiary rather than the decision-maker and hero
- Content that sounds like it was written for a search engine rather than a person
- Missing FAQ sections (every post needs at least 3 FAQ questions)
- Missing internal links to the relevant service pages

**How to submit work:** Deliver posts in Markdown format with the full frontmatter block (title, description, date, tags, author, featured). The date should be the date of delivery. Spencer will review, approve, and publish.
