Richmond has a visible mural culture and no shortage of people willing to paint walls. The range of quality, professionalism, and durability in the work reflects that.

Here is what separates a mural that holds up for 20 years from one that fades in three — and how to identify which kind of artist you are talking to before you sign anything.

## Mural work is not house painting

This sounds obvious. In practice, clients regularly conflate them.

A professional muralist needs fine art skill to execute complex imagery at scale, architectural awareness to understand how a design reads from 20 feet away versus 2 feet away, surface chemistry knowledge to understand which paints adhere to which substrates and why, and project management ability to execute work on a building that may still be occupied.

The stakes are visible. A mural failure is public. It is on the side of your building. It costs real money to fix — either repainting or covering the entire wall. The selection decision is worth spending time on.

## What to evaluate in a portfolio

Looking at a portfolio and thinking "that looks nice" is not sufficient evaluation.

Ask yourself these questions:

**Scale.** Has the artist executed work at the scale you need? A muralist who primarily works on 5x5-foot interior pieces is not necessarily ready for a 20x40-foot exterior wall. Ask about the largest project they have completed.

**Surfaces.** Have they worked on the substrate you have? Brick, smooth stucco, concrete block, metal panel, and wood all behave differently. An artist who has only worked on smooth drywall is starting from scratch on exterior masonry.

**Exterior work.** Outdoor murals require different paints, different prep, different sealants, and different execution under variable weather conditions. Indoor and outdoor experience are not equivalent.

**Longevity evidence.** Anyone can photograph a mural the day it is completed. Ask to see photos of completed work from two, five, or ten years ago. Does it still look correct? Or has it faded, peeled, or bubbled? Longevity is the real proof of a professional.

**Professional documentation.** Phone photos from a passing car are not a portfolio. Professional photos under good lighting, at multiple scales, showing the full wall and detail areas, indicate that the artist treats their work as professional output worth documenting correctly.

## Ask about insurance before you ask about price

General liability insurance matters for mural work. Ladders, scaffolding, wet paint on a public-facing surface, and work happening on or near a building that may still have occupants create real liability exposure. If an accident happens during installation, you want to know that the artist carries coverage.

Many muralists — particularly newer or part-time painters — do not carry general liability insurance. This is not a disqualifying condition for every project, but it is information you should have before work begins. A muralist who carries proper coverage has made a professional commitment that casual painters have not.

Ask directly: "Do you carry general liability insurance, and can you provide a certificate?" A professional will have a current certificate readily available.

## Understand the process before you commit

The process a muralist follows before picking up a brush reveals how they will handle problems during execution.

**Site visit.** A professional does not price or design a mural without visiting the wall. The site visit is when measurements are taken, the substrate is assessed, access requirements are evaluated, and the lighting and visibility angles are understood. A muralist who quotes remotely from a photo has skipped the information they need to do the job correctly.

**Design and revision process.** How does the artist present the design? A sketch on paper is one data point. A photorealistic render showing the design on your actual wall, at actual scale, is a different level of certainty. Revision rounds should be defined — how many, at what stage, what happens if you want a change after work begins.

**Written proposal.** Scope, timeline, payment schedule, revision policy, what happens if the project goes over timeline. These should be in writing before work starts, not discussed informally and remembered differently by both parties.

**Surface preparation.** Ask what the prep process looks like for your specific surface. The answer tells you whether the artist understands surface chemistry or is planning to paint over whatever is already there.

**Red flags:** No site visit. No written proposal. No revision policy. No timeline. Vague answers about materials. Requests for full payment upfront.

## Materials matter more than most clients realize

Ask your muralist what paint they plan to use and why. This is not a trick question — it is basic professional information that any experienced muralist should answer fluently.

For exterior murals on masonry, the professional options are KEIM mineral silicate paint (which chemically bonds to mineral substrates and effectively becomes part of the wall) and high-quality exterior acrylics like Nova Color or Golden Mural Paint (which have the pigment density and lightfastness ratings for outdoor use). House paint from a hardware store has neither.

For interior work, artist-grade acrylics with high pigment loads produce better color retention than standard interior latex. The difference is visible within a few years.

Ask about primers and why they chose them for your specific substrate. Ask about sealants and UV protection. A muralist who cannot name specific products and explain the reasoning behind them has not thought carefully about the technical requirements of the work.

## The contract

A mural contract should define: scope of work, timeline, payment schedule, what happens if weather or access delays the schedule, revision policy and at what stages revisions are allowed, the artist's VARA rights and under what conditions those rights are modified, what maintenance the owner is responsible for, and how disputes are handled.

This is not adversarial. It is the document that prevents misunderstandings from becoming disputes. If an artist resists having these terms in writing, that is information.

## Richmond-specific considerations

Richmond's mural culture has produced a lot of visible painted work across the city, and there is genuine talent here. There is also work that will not look the same in five years.

Richmond's climate creates specific technical requirements: the humidity drives adhesion failure in work with inadequate surface prep, the UV intensity in summer is harsher than many painters from cooler climates expect, and brick is the dominant surface in many commercial neighborhoods, which requires specific primers and paint chemistry.

A muralist who works regularly in Richmond should know these conditions. Ask about their experience with Richmond's climate and specific substrates. The answer reveals how much they have thought about the long-term performance of their work.
