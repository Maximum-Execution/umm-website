## Why this decision matters more than it looks

Businesses with murals see 7 to 20 percent more foot traffic and 12 percent higher sales compared to businesses without public art (OAAA). Property values within one block of a well-executed mural increase by up to 15 percent. A mural is not decoration. It is an investment that changes how people find, remember, and walk into your business.

The investment case is clear. What is less clear is how wide the gap in quality runs among the people available to do the work.

Richmond has no licensing requirement for muralists, no regulatory body, and no barrier to entry. Anyone with a brush and a social media account can present themselves as a professional. Walk through Carytown, Scott's Addition, Church Hill, or the Broad Street corridor and you'll see work ranging from technically masterful to visibly failing within a few years of completion: paint bubbling off unprimed brick, colors faded to ghosts of themselves on south-facing walls, seams where vinyl was used instead of paint. The selection decision you make before signing anything determines whether your wall becomes a 20-year landmark or a five-year liability.

## Know what your wall is supposed to do

Write down two or three sentences describing what you want someone to feel and do when they see your wall before you look at a single portfolio. That statement becomes the compass that guides every subsequent decision: artist selection, design direction, color, and scale.

A mural visible from a moving vehicle needs scale and legibility. A mural experienced at close range in a restaurant needs detail and atmosphere. A mural designed for Instagram needs a strong focal point at eye level and colors that reproduce well on a phone screen. A mural designed for brand storytelling needs narrative depth and a composition that rewards sustained attention. These are different design briefs, and a muralist who doesn't ask which one you need is designing without a compass.

## How to evaluate a muralist's portfolio and skills

Looking at a portfolio and thinking "that looks nice" is not sufficient evaluation. The portfolio review should answer four specific questions, each tied to a skill that separates serious mural work from house painting.

**Can they work at your scale?** A mural is not an enlarged small drawing. Representing form, depth, value, and color across surfaces measured in dozens or hundreds of square feet requires spatial intelligence and physical problem-solving that small-scale work does not develop. Artists who have not practiced at large scale produce distorted proportions on a wall. This is a documented, recurring complaint in mural commission reviews. Ask: "What is the largest project you have completed, and how did you scale the design?" Then look at the work. A 5-by-5-foot interior piece and a 20-by-40-foot exterior wall require related but different skills.

**Have they worked on your surface?** Brick, concrete, smooth drywall, stucco, and metal all require different primers, paints, and preparation protocols. Mistakes in surface preparation don't appear on completion day. They show up two years later, as bubbling, fading, or peeling. Ask to see documented examples on your specific surface. An artist with excellent drywall technique is not automatically competent on historic brick.

**Do they have exterior experience (if your project is exterior)?** Outdoor murals require UV-resistant paints with appropriate fade-resistance ratings, masonry-compatible primers, anti-graffiti sealants, and the logistical capacity to manage work under variable weather conditions. Interior and outdoor experience are not equivalent. If your project is exterior, ask specifically about outdoor work and how many exterior murals they've completed in humid climates.

**Can they show you work that has aged?** Anyone can photograph a mural the day it is completed. Ask to see photos of work from two, five, or ten years ago. Does the color still read correctly, or has it faded, cracked, or bubbled? Longevity is the real proof of an experienced muralist. An artist who cannot show aging work either hasn't been working long enough or hasn't been tracking the durability of their installations.

One more thing to evaluate: style range. Every muralist has a native style. An artist who paints abstract color fields but accepts a photorealistic portraiture commission is working outside their training. The professional response is to decline or refer. The red flag is accepting and attempting it anyway.

## Richmond's specific technical requirements

Richmond is not a generic American city for paint purposes. It has a specific climate, specific building stock, and a specific regulatory environment that make local knowledge a requirement, not an advantage.

### The climate

Richmond sits in a humid subtropical zone (USDA Plant Hardiness Zone 7b). Summer highs average 89 to 90 degrees in July, with morning humidity that can reach 90 percent. The UV index peaks at 9 to 10 from May through August, classified as "very high." Annual precipitation averages 47 inches across roughly 154 rain days per year.

This combination punishes exterior coatings applied without appropriate preparation. Moisture migrates through masonry, humidity prevents proper cure times, and high UV accelerates fading of inadequately rated pigments. The optimal installation window for exterior murals in Richmond is spring (April to early June) and early fall (mid-September to October), with temperatures between 50 and 85 degrees and relative humidity below 70 percent. A muralist who does not account for Richmond's seasonal conditions is planning to install under conditions that compromise the work from day one.

### Brick is the dominant commercial surface

The neighborhoods where murals are most often commissioned (Carytown, the Fan, Jackson Ward, Church Hill, Shockoe Bottom, Manchester, Scott's Addition) are defined by 19th and early 20th century brick. Brick actively transmits moisture vapor and requires a masonry-compatible bonding primer before any topcoat. Paint applied over unprimed or inadequately primed brick blisters as moisture migrates through and lifts the paint from beneath.

Mineral silicate paint bonds to brick at the chemical level. The paint soaks into the masonry and hardens into part of the wall itself. It can't peel or flake because it's not sitting on top of the surface. It IS the surface. These systems let more moisture pass through than the brick itself does (three times more breathable than standard brick), which means the wall can still breathe. Documented durability exceeds 100 years. For exterior masonry murals expected to last a generation, this distinction matters.

### Historic district requirements

Richmond has 122 designated historic districts encompassing nearly 28,000 properties. The Commission of Architectural Review (CAR), established in 1957, requires a Certificate of Appropriateness before exterior alterations, including mural installation, on properties within these districts. Mural designs in historic areas may require a public hearing before the Commission.

Ask any prospective muralist directly: "Are you familiar with CAR requirements and the permit process for historic districts?" Their answer reveals whether they have worked professionally in this city's regulatory environment.

## Ask about insurance before you ask about price

General liability insurance for mural painting businesses costs an average of $300 to $600 per year for $1 million in coverage. That is a modest business expense. An artist who does not carry it has made a choice that tells you something.

Ladders, lift equipment, wet paint on a public-facing surface, and work on an occupied commercial building create real liability exposure. If a ladder damages your storefront window, if a painter is injured on your property, if wet paint contacts a customer's vehicle, the question of who pays depends on insurance coverage. Without it, liability falls on the property owner.

Ask directly: "Do you carry general liability insurance, and can you provide a certificate of insurance before we begin?" A professional will have a current certificate readily available. An artist who is confused by the question or unable to produce documentation has answered it.

## Materials matter more than most clients realize

Ask your muralist what paint they plan to use and why. Any experienced muralist should answer this without hesitating. The paint system determines how long the mural lasts, how colors retain vibrancy, and whether the wall underneath stays healthy. [What makes a mural last](/journal/what-makes-a-mural-last) covers materials in full detail.

**For exterior murals on masonry:** Mineral silicate paint is the best option for brick because it bonds into the wall at the chemical level and can't peel. For surfaces where mineral paint isn't the right fit, high-quality exterior acrylic mural paints (formulated with artist-grade pigments rated for outdoor fade resistance under ASTM D4303) are a major step up from standard hardware store paint. Standard exterior latex doesn't have the color depth or the fade resistance for a long-term mural on a south-facing Richmond wall.

**For interior murals:** Artist-grade acrylics with high pigment loads produce much better color retention than standard interior latex. The difference is visible within a few years. [Interior murals vs wallpaper](/journal/interior-murals-vs-wallpaper) covers the material comparison in detail.

**Primers and topcoats** are not afterthoughts. Ask about the primer for your wall type and why it was chosen. Ask about the topcoat's UV rating. For commercial applications, ask whether it provides cleanability. For exterior murals vulnerable to vandalism, ask whether a sacrificial anti-graffiti coating is included.

A muralist who cannot name specific products and explain why they chose them for your surface has not thought through the technical requirements of the work.

## The contract

A mural contract is not a sign of distrust. It is the document that converts a verbal understanding into a shared written record. A professional mural contract should define:

**Scope of work.** Exactly what surfaces, imagery, preparation, and sealants are included, referencing the approved design by version.

**Timeline.** Start date, completion date, and what happens if weather or access creates delays.

**Payment schedule.** Deposit amount (typically 33 to 50 percent), milestone payments if applicable, and the final payment trigger.

**Revision policy.** How many rounds are included, at what stages, and what scope changes cost.

**Design approval.** Written confirmation required before any painting begins.

**Materials specification.** Specific paint brands and primer system named in writing.

**VARA rights.** A written waiver specifying that the artist waives the right to prevent modification, alteration, or removal of the work. Without this waiver, federal copyright law (the Visual Artists Rights Act of 1990) may give the artist the right to block renovation or demolition of the building. This risk produced a $6.75 million federal judgment in New York in 2018.

**Maintenance responsibilities and dispute resolution.**

If a muralist resists having any of these terms in writing, that resistance is itself information.

## Red flags that predict failure

The mural industry's three most common complaint patterns are documented across client reviews, Reddit forums, and industry publications. Knowing them before you hire protects you from repeating what hundreds of other clients have learned the hard way.

### Communication collapse

Communication collapse tops every complaint list across every platform. The muralist who is slow to respond before the contract is signed will be slower during the project. The pattern escalates: days between responses become weeks. Progress updates stop. The client discovers that work has drifted from the approved design without being consulted. One documented Reddit thread captured this pattern: commissioners either don't get updates until they demand a refund, or they get outright lies about progress.

A muralist who commits to a specific response time (24 hours during the project, for example) and sends unprompted progress updates has already differentiated from the majority of the market on this dimension alone.

### The gap between portfolio and delivery

This complaint generates the most public damage because it produces the photos of failed murals that circulate in community Facebook groups for years. The pattern is consistent: a client finds an artist whose portfolio shows polished work. The work delivered doesn't match.

Three things drive this gap. First, curated portfolio deception: the portfolio reflects the artist's best work under ideal conditions, not their typical output. Second, scale incompetence: producing beautiful small-scale work is a different skill from scaling accurately to a wall. Third, style-matching failure: the artist accepts a project outside their native style and the result satisfies no one.

A muralist who documents their process (in-progress photos, video of the painting sequence) rather than only showing finished results provides evidence of skill that a curated gallery of final photos cannot fake.

### Projects that never fully end

The abandonment scenario has a consistent structure across documented cases. The artist takes a deposit. Progress slows. Communication becomes evasive. Eventually the artist stops responding, leaving the client with a half-painted wall, an unrecoverable deposit, and a damaged surface that requires repair.

The related problem is "finished but not really": the artist declares completion before all agreed elements are done, before touch-ups are addressed, or before the final clear coat is applied. The artist disappears with the final payment, leaving visible issues the client either lives with or pays to fix.

Protect yourself with milestone-based payments tied to specific deliverables (design approval, prep completion, painting completion), not lump-sum deposits.

## The question that separates the professionals

You've reviewed portfolios, verified insurance, understood the process, and evaluated materials knowledge. Now ask one final question:

**"Can I speak to a client whose mural is at least five years old?"**

A professional with a real track record in Richmond will provide this reference immediately. Visit the wall yourself. Look for chalking (a powdery residue on the surface that indicates the paint system is breaking down). Check for color shift, especially on south-facing walls where UV exposure is highest. Inspect the edges where the mural meets unpainted surface for lifting or separation. Run your hand along the surface: a healthy mural feels smooth and integrated with the wall. A failing mural feels papery, gritty, or loose.

Surface chemistry failures (bubbling, peeling, significant UV fading) typically take two to three years to appear. A five-year-old wall reveals the truth about an artist's understanding of surface preparation and material durability.

An artist who cannot provide this reference either hasn't been working long enough, or has been working long enough but prefers you don't see the aging work. Either answer tells you something important.
