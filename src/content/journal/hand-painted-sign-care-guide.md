A hand-painted sign is built to outlast any vinyl or printed alternative. Most of the ones painted a century ago are still visible today — faded, but intact. The ones that failed early were not poorly painted. They were poorly maintained.

The good news: maintaining a hand-painted sign is simple, infrequent, and inexpensive compared to replacing one. Here is what you actually need to do.

## Cleaning: what to use, what to avoid

A hand-painted sign collects the same grime as any exterior surface: road film, pollen, bird droppings, and in some locations, mildew. Cleaning once or twice a year is usually enough to keep it looking correct.

**What to use:** A soft cloth or natural sponge, warm water, and a small amount of mild dish soap. Wipe gently in the direction of the brushwork where possible. Rinse thoroughly and let the sign dry naturally.

For stubborn grime — dried bird droppings, mildew, road tar — a diluted all-purpose cleaner applied with a soft cloth and left to soak for a minute before wiping will handle most situations without damaging the paint.

**What not to do:** Never pressure wash a hand-painted sign. This is the most common and most damaging mistake sign owners make. Pressure washing strips paint from edges, drives water into wood grain or masonry, lifts sealant, and can cause delamination of the entire paint film. There is no pressure setting that is safe for a painted sign.

Do not use abrasive scrubbers, steel wool, or scouring pads. They scratch the clear coat and create micro-abrasions that trap moisture and accelerate fading.

Do not use acetone, mineral spirits, or solvent-based cleaners near painted lettering. These solvents will soften the paint binder and blur the lettering edges. They can also strip gold leaf completely.

## When to reseal

Every exterior hand-painted sign should have a clear coat applied on top of the finish — typically a UV-protective spar varnish, marine-grade polyurethane, or specialty mural varnish, depending on the substrate and paint used.

That clear coat degrades over time from UV exposure. When it fails, the paint beneath it becomes directly exposed to weather, which accelerates fading and moisture damage.

**How to tell when resealing is needed:** Run your finger across a section of the sign that gets direct sun. If the surface feels chalky, rough, or significantly duller than it looked when new, the clear coat has degraded and it is time to reseal.

For exterior signs in full sun exposure, inspect annually and expect to reseal every 2–4 years. Signs on north-facing walls or under awnings need less frequent resealing — sometimes every 5–7 years. Interior signs may never need resealing.

Resealing is straightforward: clean the surface thoroughly, let it dry completely (48–72 hours in Richmond's humidity), and apply a fresh coat of the appropriate clear coat. If you did not paint the sign yourself, contact the original painter — they will know what product was used and can ensure compatibility.

## Sun damage and what you can do about it

All paint fades in direct sunlight. This is physics, not a sign of poor quality. The rate of fading depends on which pigments were used, how thick the paint film is, and whether a UV-protective sealant is intact.

Some pigments fade faster than others. Warm colors — cadmium reds, benzimidazolone yellows, dioxazine violets — are more susceptible to UV damage than cool colors. A professionally painted sign should use only high-lightfastness pigments for outdoor work, which significantly extends the period before visible fading occurs.

**Preventive measures.** An intact UV-protective clear coat is your primary defense. Keeping up with the resealing schedule matters more than almost anything else you can do.

If you are choosing a location for a new sign, morning sun is easier on paint than afternoon western exposure. A south-facing sign that catches direct sun from 10 AM to sunset degrades faster than one in partial shade or on a north-facing wall. If you have a choice, factor this in.

**When fading has already occurred.** Localized fading on warm-color areas can often be addressed with a targeted touch-up from the original painter. This requires matching the exact pigment formulation, which is why maintaining the relationship with whoever painted the sign is valuable.

## When to call for a touch-up

Hand-painted signs are not one-and-done. They are a long-lived material object, and like all material objects, they benefit from periodic professional attention.

**Signs that indicate a touch-up is needed:**
- Visible color shift or fading in one or more areas
- Hairline cracks in the paint film, particularly at letter edges
- Minor chipping at corners or edges from physical contact
- The sealant has been reapplied over an area where the underlying paint has shifted

A touch-up at year five or eight can extend a sign's useful life by another five or ten years. It is significantly cheaper than repainting the whole sign. Contact the original painter if possible — color matching requires knowing the original pigment formulation, and a painter who did not paint the sign originally is working blind.

## Common mistakes that shorten sign life

**Pressure washing.** Strips paint, lifts sealant, drives water into wood. Do not do it.

**Mounting without standoffs.** A sign mounted flush against a wall or door traps moisture behind it. That moisture has nowhere to go. It wicks into the wood substrate, causes rot, and eventually destroys adhesion from the back. Standoffs hold the sign slightly away from the surface, allowing air circulation.

**Ignoring the resealing schedule.** UV damage accelerates once the clear coat fails. A sign that went years without resealing often requires more extensive touch-up or repainting than one kept on a regular schedule.

**Cleaning with harsh chemicals.** Bleach, acetone, and solvent-based cleaners can strip paint binder, dull gold leaf, and eat through clear coat. Mild soap and water handle 95 percent of cleaning situations.

**No air circulation.** Signs in enclosed recessed spaces with no airflow trap moisture and develop mildew faster. If the mounting location has poor air circulation, inspect more frequently and clean more often.

A hand-painted sign maintained on this schedule will outlast every vinyl sign installed in the same year. The craft is old because it works. Treat the sign correctly and it will be there long after its digital alternatives have been replaced three times.
