Most people who commission a mural have never done it before. The first question is usually about cost. The second, once they know the price, is some version of: "How does this actually work?"

A professional mural project moves through six phases: site visit, surface preparation, design and approval, layout transfer, painting, and sealing. Every professional muralist follows some version of this sequence. The difference between a mural that lasts 20 years and one that starts peeling in two lies in the care put into each phase.

This post walks you through all six steps so you know what to expect, what questions to ask, and what warning signs to watch for.

---

## Step 1: The site visit finds problems before they cost you.

A wall cannot be evaluated from a photograph. Photographs flatten depth, hide surface texture, and eliminate the viewing angles that determine how the mural reads from the street.

A professional muralist visits the site to measure the wall, assess the substrate (brick, stucco, concrete, drywall), evaluate sun exposure, and study where people actually stand when they look at this wall. A south-facing exterior wall in Richmond's summer heat is a completely different painting environment than a north-facing wall under a canopy. The materials, the timeline, and the design approach all change based on what the site visit reveals.

The muralist should also check for problems that will cause the mural to fail if left unaddressed: cracks, efflorescence (white mineral deposits that push paint off masonry), water damage, and layers of failing old paint. Finding these problems before quoting saves both parties from expensive surprises during installation.

## Step 2: Surface prep decides if your mural lasts 2 years or 20.

Surface preparation determines whether the mural lasts two years or twenty. It is the invisible foundation of the entire project.

A mural is only as strong as its bond to the wall. That bond depends on the primer, and the primer depends on the substrate. Drywall, brick, concrete, stucco, and previously painted surfaces all absorb paint differently and require different primer systems. Using the wrong primer is like gluing wood with the wrong adhesive: it holds for a while, then separates under stress.

**What every professional muralist should do:** Clean the wall to remove dirt, grease, biological growth, and loose material. Repair cracks and damaged areas. Apply primer matched to the specific substrate. Allow full curing time before painting begins.

**Exterior masonry requires the most prep.** Pressure washing comes first, followed by treating any efflorescence with an acid wash, repairing damaged mortar joints, and applying masonry-specific primer. In humid climates like Richmond's, the wall needs 48 to 72 hours of drying time after washing before any primer goes on. Rushing this step traps moisture under the primer, which causes the paint to bubble and peel within the first year.

**Interior surfaces** need less work: cleaning, light sanding where the surface is glossy, and bonding primer where adhesion is a concern.

This step adds a day or two to the timeline. It is the most important day or two of the entire project.

**What Untitled Mixed Media does differently:** I perform adhesion tests on questionable surfaces before committing to a primer system. I match every primer to the specific substrate: PVA primer for new drywall, penetrating masonry primer for brick and concrete, bonding primer for previously painted surfaces. The primer creates the chemical bond that holds the mural to the wall for decades.

## Step 3: You see the design before any paint is mixed.

The design phase is where the mural takes shape before any paint is mixed. It is also where the biggest financial risk lives: painting the wrong design on a wall is expensive to fix. Every dollar spent on rigorous pre-visualization saves ten dollars in rework.

**The minimum standard:** Every professional muralist should provide some form of design preview before painting starts. This might be a pencil sketch, a digital illustration, or a photo composite showing the design superimposed on a photograph of the wall.

**The limitation of flat previews:** A flat image pasted onto a wall photo doesn't account for perspective distortion, real-world scale, or how the mural reads from actual viewing angles. A design that looks balanced on a laptop screen can look distorted on the wall because the proportions don't translate from a flat rectangle to a three-dimensional surface with depth, curves, and architectural features.

**What Untitled Mixed Media does differently:** I build a digital twin of your wall from the photographs collected during the site visit. The twin captures the actual surface geometry, its surroundings, and its real-world lighting. I place the mural design onto this digital twin and render out what it will look like under different lighting conditions. You see the finished mural on your actual wall, from the actual viewing angle, before any paint is mixed.

Design revisions happen at this stage. A color change in the digital model takes minutes. The same change on a painted wall takes a day. This is why rigorous pre-visualization saves money: it catches problems before they become permanent.

**The approval gate:** Nothing should be painted until you approve the design in writing. This protects both you and the muralist. The approval should specify the design, the colors, the dimensions, and the timeline. Most professional studios include two to three rounds of revision in the project price. Once approved, the design is locked. Changes after this point involve real rework cost.

"Looked nothing like the concept" is one of the most common complaints in mural client reviews. A rigorous design preview and a written approval step make that complaint structurally impossible. If you approved a photorealistic render and the painted result matches it, there is no gap between expectation and delivery.

## Step 4: The design goes from screen to wall.

The approved design has to get from the preview onto the wall at full scale. Four methods are common, and each has a specific use case.

**Grid transfer** is the traditional method. The design is divided into a proportional grid. The same grid is chalked onto the wall at the correct scale. Forms are drawn freehand within each grid square. This method works on uneven surfaces, wraps around architectural features, and needs no electricity. The result looks genuinely hand-painted rather than traced.

**Projection** uses a high-lumen digital projector to trace the design directly onto the wall. It works best in interior environments with controlled lighting and enough throw distance between the projector and the wall. Faster than grid transfer for complex or photorealistic compositions, but it requires power and dim conditions.

**Pounce** is a historical technique (Michelangelo used a version of it on the Sistine Chapel ceiling). A full-scale paper pattern is perforated with a spiked wheel, and chalk is patted through the holes to leave dotted outlines on the wall. Ideal for typography, logos, and designs that require exact registration.

**Doodle wall** is a quick transfer method for situations where a projector has no power source, a grid takes too long, or the wind makes pounce paper unmanageable. The muralist draws an abstract arrangement of doodles on the wall that serve as registration points. Then they overlay their artwork on a phone or computer to check alignment and adjust. One person can do it fast with no equipment. It's the most practical option when conditions won't cooperate with other methods.

The layout is a starting point in all cases. The actual line quality and paint decisions happen during the painting phase.

## Step 5: The wall transforms from blank to finished.

Mural painting follows a consistent sequence regardless of the muralist or the style: background to foreground, large to small, rough to refined.

**Background blocking** covers the wall first. Large color areas go down to establish the composition's major shapes and eliminate the blank wall. For clients watching the process, this is the phase that feels slowest relative to what's visible. The muralist is covering area, not creating detail. It looks like broad strokes of color, and that's correct.

**Mid-detail development** builds the forms, refines the shapes, and establishes the color relationships between elements. The image gains coherence in this phase. Shadows appear. Depth emerges. For most clients, this is the moment the project stops being abstract and starts looking like a real mural. It's also the phase where the muralist makes the most consequential decisions about color temperature, edge quality, and spatial depth. These choices determine whether the mural feels flat or dimensional from the viewing distance.

**Detail work** adds fine lines, highlights, textures, and typography. Focal elements like faces, intricate branding, and key imagery are saved for the final passes. These elements carry the most emotional weight and need to be perfectly integrated into the composition that's already established around them. Painting them too early risks having to repaint surrounding areas, which wastes time and can compromise color consistency.

**Touch-ups and evaluation** happen after the paint has had time to dry. The muralist steps back to the normal viewing distance and evaluates the entire composition as a whole. Edge corrections, color adjustments, and final refinements happen in this pass. Fresh eyes matter: details that looked right from two feet away can read differently from across the street.

## Step 6: Your mural gets sealed, documented, and handed off.

The final coat of clear varnish or UV-protective sealant goes on after the paint has fully dried, usually 24 to 48 hours after the last paint layer. The sealant stands between the paint and the elements. It is not optional.

**Archival varnish** unifies the sheen (gloss or matte) and provides a wipeable surface for interior commercial spaces. It also adds a layer of UV protection that slows color fading.

**UV-protective clear coat** is standard for exterior murals. Most exterior work gets two coats for full coverage. The sealant shields pigments from the UV radiation that breaks down color molecules over time.

**Anti-graffiti coating** goes on street-facing exterior murals. Sacrificial coatings can be cleaned and reapplied without affecting the mural underneath. Permanent non-stick coatings allow spray paint and markers to be wiped away without touching the sealant layer.

**Documentation and handoff.** Every completed mural should be professionally photographed, and the client should receive a maintenance guide: how to clean the mural, when to inspect the sealant, signs that indicate touch-up is needed, and the recommended schedule for sealant reapplication. The mural can look correct for 20 years. That outcome requires that the owner knows how to maintain it.

When someone asks about the mural on your wall, you have the documentation to explain exactly why it looks the way it does.

## How long does a mural take?

Timelines vary by size, complexity, and surface condition. These benchmarks cover the painting phase only (design and surface prep are additional time):

- Small (under 100 sqft): 1 to 3 days
- Medium (100 to 400 sqft): 3 to 7 days
- Large (400 to 1,000 sqft): 1 to 2 weeks
- Major public commissions (1,000+ sqft): 2 to 6 weeks

The first day of installation often involves zero visible painting. That day is spent on final prep, layout transfer, and establishing the base layer. Clients who expect to see a mural taking shape on day one should know this upfront.

Total project timelines from first conversation to finished mural run 3 to 12 weeks for most projects. The design phase and surface prep complexity are the biggest variables. A simple interior accent wall with a clean surface can move from concept to completion in three weeks. A large exterior mural on weathered brick with a complex design can take three months.

## How to spot an unprofessional muralist

The mural industry has no licensing requirement, no regulatory body, and no barrier to entry. Anyone who buys a brush and creates a social media account can present themselves as a professional. The complaints that show up across Reddit, Google reviews, and industry forums cluster into three patterns that account for most negative client experiences. Here is what to watch for before, during, and after a project.

### Before you hire

**No site visit.** They quote from a photograph without visiting the wall. This means they haven't assessed the substrate, the surface condition, the sun exposure, or the viewing angles. A quote based on a photo is a guess.

**Portfolio that doesn't match what you need.** A muralist's portfolio shows their best work in their strongest style. If you want photorealistic portraiture and their portfolio shows abstract color fields, they are being asked to work outside their training. The professional response is to decline or refer. The red flag is accepting the project and attempting it anyway.

**No written contract.** The absence of a contract is the clearest signal of unprofessionalism in the industry. A contract protects both parties from disputes about scope, timeline, payment, and design revisions. A muralist who doesn't use contracts either doesn't know they should or doesn't want to be held to written commitments.

**Vague or verbal-only pricing.** If a muralist can't explain how they arrived at a price (square footage, complexity, materials, access conditions), they are either guessing or plan to change the number later. Transparent pricing is evidence of someone who has done this before.

**No reference to surface preparation.** Ask directly: "How will you prepare my wall before painting?" A muralist who says "I'll just start painting" or doesn't mention priming has told you everything about how long the finished work will last.

**No discussion of materials.** Ask what paint they'll use. A professional names a specific brand, explains why it's appropriate for the surface and conditions, and speaks with confidence about durability. A muralist who says "I'll use whatever I can find" is not equipped for the job.

**Fake or suspicious online reviews.** Warning signs include: reviews from accounts with no prior review history, reviews that mention generic praise but no project specifics, reviews clustered in a short time period, and reviewer accounts with no location. This pattern is documented specifically in the painting and home services industry.

### During the project

**No design approval step.** If a muralist starts painting before getting written approval of the design, you've lost your ability to course-correct. Any complaint about the design becomes difficult to enforce because there is no documented agreement.

**The design drifts without notice.** Changes to color, composition, scale, or content that weren't discussed are a contract violation if design approval was specified. More importantly, they reveal that the muralist is prioritizing their preferences over your agreed vision.

**Communication goes silent.** This is the single most recurring complaint across every platform. The muralist who is slow to respond before the contract is signed will be slower during the project. If messages go unanswered during installation, the client is managing the early stages of an abandonment scenario. A professional muralist responds within 24 hours and sends unprompted progress updates.

**Rushed timeline under pressure.** Compressed timelines produce rushed work. If three weeks of work gets squeezed into one, quality collapses. Clients who push unrealistic timelines and muralists who accept them are both creating the conditions for failure.

### After the project

**No protective topcoat.** A mural without a clear varnish or sealant is an incomplete delivery. The topcoat provides the cleanable surface that makes the work practical in a commercial environment. Omitting it is either a scoping failure or a sign that the muralist ran out of time and didn't tell you.

**No walkthrough or punch list.** A muralist who leaves before doing a final walkthrough with the client, before confirming the sealant is fully cured, and before addressing any remaining touch-ups has not completed the project. They have stopped painting. Those are different things.

**No documentation at handoff.** Final photography, a maintenance guide, and a written record of materials used form the close of a professional engagement. Without these, you have no basis for future touch-ups, no record of what was delivered, and no way to maintain the work properly.

**The deposit-and-disappear pattern.** Particularly damaging: the muralist receives a deposit, produces minimal work or none, and becomes unreachable. This is documented frequently enough across Reddit and review platforms to be considered an industry-recognized hazard, not an anomaly. Protect yourself with milestone-based payments tied to specific deliverables (design approval, prep completion, painting completion), not lump-sum deposits.
