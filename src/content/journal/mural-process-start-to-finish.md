Most clients have never commissioned a mural before. The first question is almost always about cost, but the second — once cost has been addressed — is usually some version of: "How does this actually work?"

Here is the complete answer, step by step.

## Step 1: The site visit

Nothing meaningful about a mural project can be determined from a photo. The site visit is where the actual work begins.

On the first visit, I measure the wall precisely. I assess the substrate — is this smooth stucco, textured brick, painted concrete, or something else? What is the current condition? Are there cracks, efflorescence, areas of failing paint? I evaluate the sun exposure. A south-facing wall in Richmond's summer sun is a different environment than a north-facing wall under a canopy. I look at the visibility angles: where do people actually stand when they look at this wall? From the sidewalk directly across the street? From a moving car? From 50 feet away?

I also photograph the wall extensively — hundreds of photos from multiple angles and distances. This is not for reference. These photos are the raw material for the next step.

## Step 2: Photogrammetry and 3D capture

This is the part that separates UMM's pre-visualization from a sketch on paper or a flat digital mockup pasted onto a photo.

The site photos are processed through RealityCapture, a photogrammetry application, to generate a three-dimensional point cloud model of the wall and its immediate surroundings. The software stitches hundreds of photos into a spatially accurate 3D representation of the actual surface: its curves, its offsets, its surrounding architecture.

That 3D model is then imported into Blender. Using fSpy for camera matching, the model is aligned to a specific real-world viewpoint — typically the primary viewing angle from the street or sidewalk. This means that when the design is placed on the wall in 3D space, it will appear in the final render exactly as it will appear on the actual wall when you stand in front of it. Correct perspective, correct scale, correct foreshortening.

Most muralists show you a flat digital image superimposed over a photo of your wall. What I show you is a 3D render of your actual wall, with your actual mural on it, viewed from the angle you will actually see it from.

## Step 3: Design and pre-visualization

The design process starts before the Blender work. Research, references, mood boards, and compositional sketches come first. For heritage or community murals, this includes source research: archival photos, historical records, interviews with stakeholders.

Once the concept is developed to a stage where the composition and imagery are clear, it moves into Blender. The design is painted onto the 3D wall model using a dual UV paint system that allows precise color placement. The render is then processed through Magnific for upscaling and color-graded in DaVinci Resolve.

The result is a photorealistic image of what your mural will look like on your actual wall, before any paint has been mixed. You can evaluate the colors, the composition, the scale, and how the mural reads from the primary viewing angle.

Revision rounds happen at this stage. Changes are inexpensive in software. A color revision in Blender takes minutes. The same revision on a painted wall is a day's work.

## Step 4: Client approval

Nothing is painted until the design is approved in writing.

This gate is not bureaucratic — it is the point of the entire pre-visualization process. The render exists so you can evaluate the design with full information before any irreversible paint hits the wall. If the design is not right, the revision process is still open.

Once the design is approved, the project moves into production. The design is locked. Changes after this point — if required — involve real rework cost.

## Step 5: Surface preparation

Surface preparation is the invisible foundation of mural longevity. A mural painted over inadequate prep will fail. The prep work is not photographable, does not appear in the finished piece, and is the part of the project that most distinguishes professional work from amateurish work.

For exterior masonry: pressure washing to remove biological contamination and loose material, then full drying time (48–72 hours in Richmond's humidity). Any efflorescence is treated with a mineral acid wash. Cracks and mortar joints are repaired. Masonry conditioner or bonding primer is applied and allowed to cure fully before any design work starts.

For interior surfaces: cleaning, sanding if needed, bonding primer where adhesion is a concern.

This step can add a day or two to the timeline. It is not skippable.

## Step 6: Layout transfer

Getting the design from the render onto the wall requires a method of transferring the composition at full scale. The right method depends on the project.

**Grid transfer** is the traditional approach. The design is divided into a grid. The corresponding grid is chalked onto the wall at the correct scale. Forms are drawn in freehand within each grid square. This method is appropriate for most compositions and produces work that looks genuinely painted rather than traced.

**Projection** is faster for complex compositions or photorealistic work where precise reference is critical. A projector is used to project the design reference onto the wall in dim conditions. The forms are sketched. The projector is removed. This is a tool, not a shortcut — the painting that follows still requires the same craft as grid transfer.

In both cases, the chalk sketch on the wall is a starting point, not a finished drawing. The actual line quality of the lettering and illustration is established during the painting.

## Step 7: Painting

Large-scale mural painting is physical work. For exterior walls requiring scaffolding or lift access, the logistics are real — staging equipment, working safely at height, managing weather windows.

The painting sequence moves from large areas to small. Background colors are blocked in first, covering the full composition. Then the mid-tones, then the detail work. Final refinement — the sharpening of edges, the final glazes, the detail that registers from close range — comes last.

For murals painted in KEIM mineral silicate paint on masonry, the application process has specific requirements. KEIM is applied in multiple translucent layers, each of which must cure before the next is applied. The final color builds through the layers rather than arriving in a single coat. The result is a surface that has depth in a way that opaque acrylics do not.

## Step 8: Sealing and protection

The final coat of clear varnish or UV-protective sealant is applied after the paint has fully dried, typically 24–48 hours after the final paint layer.

For street-facing exterior murals, anti-graffiti coating is added above the clear coat. Sacrificial coatings can be cleaned and reapplied without affecting the mural beneath. Permanent non-stick coatings allow graffiti to be wiped away without any effect on the sealant layer.

The sealant is what stands between the paint and the elements. It is not optional.

## Step 9: Documentation and handoff

Every completed mural is professionally photographed in good light at multiple scales — full-wall establishing shots, mid-range views that show the full composition, and close detail shots that document the brush quality of the work.

The client receives a maintenance guide: how to clean the mural, when to inspect the sealant, signs that indicate touch-up is needed, and the recommended schedule for sealant reapplication. This documentation is part of the project, not an afterthought.

The goal is a mural that looks correct for 20 years. That outcome requires that the client knows how to care for it.
