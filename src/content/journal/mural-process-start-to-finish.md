## Step 1: The Site Visit

Explain what happens on the first visit. Measurements, surface assessment (substrate type, condition, sun exposure, visibility angles), access evaluation (scaffolding needs, lift access, parking, permits). Mention that UMM photographs the wall from multiple angles for the photogrammetry step. This is not a casual look — it is a diagnostic assessment.

## Step 2: Photogrammetry and 3D Capture

Explain the technical differentiator. Photos from the site visit are processed through RealityCapture to create a 3D model of the wall and its surroundings. Then fSpy is used for camera matching. This means the design mockup will show the mural exactly as it will appear in real life — correct perspective, correct lighting, correct scale. Most muralists show a flat sketch. UMM shows a photorealistic render of the client's actual wall. This is the section that should make the reader think: "I have not seen anyone else do this."

## Step 3: Design and Pre-Visualization

Walk through the Blender workflow. The 3D wall model becomes the canvas for a photorealistic mockup. Dual UV paint system allows precise color matching. The render is upscaled through Magnific and color-graded in DaVinci Resolve. The client sees exactly what their mural will look like before any paint touches the wall. Revision rounds happen here — changes are cheap in software and expensive on a wall.

## Step 4: Client Approval

Emphasize the gate. Nothing is painted until the client has approved the design in writing. The pre-vis render eliminates surprises. The client knows the colors, the composition, the scale. This protects both sides. Link to the process page for more detail.

## Step 5: Surface Preparation

Cover what prep looks like in practice: pressure washing, efflorescence treatment, crack and mortar repair, primer application (masonry conditioner or bonding primer depending on substrate). Explain that this step is invisible in the finished mural but determines whether it lasts 5 years or 25. Reference the materials post for deeper detail.

## Step 6: Painting

Describe the painting phase. Grid transfer or projection for layout. Blocking in large areas first, then detail work, then final refinement. Mention the paints used (KEIM mineral paint for masonry, Nova Color acrylics where appropriate). Talk about the physical reality: scaffolding, weather windows, working at height, the stamina required for large-scale work. This humanizes the process.

## Step 7: Sealing and Protection

Cover the final protective layer: clear coat selection based on surface and exposure, anti-graffiti coating for street-facing walls, UV-protective varnish. Explain that the seal coat is what stands between the paint and the elements.
