Walk through the right parts of Richmond and you will find advertisements for products that no longer exist, painted in lettering styles that predate anyone still living, on walls that have watched the city change around them for a century or more.

These are ghost signs. They are the most durable form of commercial signage ever produced, and they are the direct ancestors of every hand-painted sign working today.

## What a ghost sign is

A ghost sign is a hand-painted advertisement on a building's exterior that has faded over decades but remains at least partially visible. They are called ghost signs because they haunt the buildings they were painted on — present but diminished, legible but not entirely there.

Every ghost sign was painted by a professional sign painter working with lead-based oil paint on brick, mortar, or masonry. They were commercial work: advertisements for tobacco companies, patent medicines, dry goods, hardware, coal, and the hundreds of other products that formed the commercial economy of the late 19th and early 20th centuries.

They were not designed to last forever. They were designed to be repainted every few years as contracts renewed and products changed. The reason so many survived is an accident of materials and history.

## Why ghost signs survive

Lead-based paint bonds to masonry with remarkable tenacity. The lead pigment penetrates porous brick and mortar at a molecular level. Unlike modern water-based paints that sit on the surface, lead paint becomes part of the substrate. When the color fades, the paint itself remains.

Many ghost signs also survived because they were covered — by later signage, by added building facades, by adjacent construction — and then uncovered when those covering elements were removed. A sign that was painted over in 1955 and revealed during a 2010 renovation can look almost fresh, protected from 55 years of weathering by whatever covered it.

Richmond's relatively conservative approach to downtown demolition compared to cities that aggressively redeveloped their commercial cores in the postwar decades preserved more ghost signs than most comparable cities have. Buildings that were knocked down in other cities are still standing here, with their painted walls intact.

## The painted advertising era

The business model that produced ghost signs ran from roughly the 1880s through the 1950s. "Wall dogs" — itinerant sign painters who traveled city to city — contracted with building owners to paint advertisements on their exterior walls in exchange for a fee paid by the advertising company.

The advertising companies were primarily tobacco manufacturers, patent medicine producers, and branded goods companies that needed national distribution for their products before mass media existed. Painted wall advertisements were the national advertising medium of their era.

Richmond was a center of this industry for reasons that were not coincidental. The city's tobacco industry made it the marketing headquarters for several major tobacco brands. American Tobacco Company, Liggett and Myers, and Philip Morris all had Richmond connections. Their advertisements appear on walls across the city and throughout the surrounding region.

The lettering styles of the period — heavily ornamented display type, condensed gothic, flowing script — were standardized across the trade through copybooks and apprenticeship. A sign painter trained in Richmond would produce lettering visually consistent with work painted in Chicago or San Francisco. The styles have since become historical documents of early commercial typography.

## Notable surviving examples in Richmond

Richmond's ghost signs cluster in the commercial neighborhoods that maintained their building stock through the 20th century.

**Shockoe Bottom and the canal district** contain some of the oldest surviving painted signage in the city, reflecting the commercial activity that centered on the James River canal system and railroad terminus in the 19th century.

**Jackson Ward and Broad Street** carry remnants of advertisements from the neighborhood's peak as a commercial and cultural center in the early 20th century.

**Manchester** on the south bank of the James has preserved examples that reflect the industrial and commercial character of the area's history.

**Church Hill** retains painted signs on the older residential-commercial buildings of its 19th-century streetscape.

Specific signs worth finding: look for tobacco advertisements and general merchandise signs on the side walls of older commercial buildings throughout the city's older neighborhoods. The best examples are typically on north or east-facing walls, where sun exposure was lower and the paint faded more slowly.

## Preservation versus restoration

There is a genuine debate among preservation advocates about what to do with ghost signs.

The pure preservation position holds that ghost signs should be left as they are — fading naturally as part of the historical record. Intervention, even careful restoration, introduces modern materials and modern hands. The sign that exists now is the authentic artifact. Restoring it creates something that looks old but is, in part, modern.

The restoration position holds that allowing a sign to continue fading until it disappears is also a choice, and a less reversible one. A carefully documented and executed restoration — using historically appropriate materials and techniques — extends the artifact's useful life without misrepresenting it as original if the restoration is clearly documented.

A third position supports painting new signs in the ghost sign tradition, treating the aesthetic vocabulary of early commercial lettering as a living craft rather than a relic. This is not preservation — it is continuation.

My own position as someone who paints signs for a living: the debate is real and the ethics are genuinely complex. A ghost sign is an artifact of a specific historical moment, and that historical character is part of its value. Restoration that papers over 100 years of weathering to make a sign look new again loses something real. But restoration that stabilizes a sign approaching illegibility, using techniques and materials consistent with the original, and documented transparently, preserves something that would otherwise disappear.

The specific sign, the condition it is in, and what is being asked of a restoration all determine what the right approach is. There is no universal answer.

## The craft lives on

Ghost signs are evidence that hand-painted lettering is not a historical curiosity. It is a technique that outlasted every commercial signage technology introduced in the century since those signs were painted.

The methods are unchanged: hand-lettered forms, oil-based paints, direct application to masonry. The materials have improved — modern mineral paints and professional acrylics outperform the original lead-based paints in UV resistance and environmental safety. But the craft itself is the same. The sign painters who painted Richmond's ghost signs would recognize what is painted on walls in the city today.

Those older signs are proof of what the technique produces over time. Not just survival — authority. The patina of weathered paint and faded lettering communicates something that no modern sign material can simulate. It says: this business has been here long enough that its sign has become part of the building.

That is not an accident of time. It is the natural outcome of painting something correctly.
