*This post is educational content about how federal law applies to mural commissions. It is not legal advice. If you have specific concerns about a mural project, consult an attorney.*

Property owners who commission murals sometimes discover — after the fact — that removing or modifying a mural is more complicated than removing a coat of paint. The reason is a federal law called the Visual Artists Rights Act. Understanding it before you sign a contract protects you and ensures the project starts with the right terms in place.

## What VARA is

The Visual Artists Rights Act of 1990 (VARA) is a federal law that grants visual artists two rights that exist separately from copyright:

**The right of attribution:** The right to be identified as the creator of a work, and to prevent false attribution.

**The right of integrity:** The right to prevent intentional destruction, mutilation, or modification of a work of recognized stature that would be prejudicial to the artist's honor or reputation.

VARA applies automatically to qualifying works. The artist does not need to register anything. The parties do not need to mention VARA in their contract for it to apply. It exists by operation of law.

## What triggers VARA protection for a mural

Not every mural qualifies for VARA's strongest protections. The key phrase is "recognized stature."

A work of recognized stature is one that has achieved a level of recognition among art experts, the community, or the media. This is not a bright-line test — there is no threshold number of Instagram followers or newspaper articles that definitively qualifies or disqualifies a mural. It is a factual determination that courts make based on the specific work and evidence of its reception.

The 5Pointz case in New York (Cohen v. G&G Realty LLC, 2020) is the most significant VARA case involving murals. The building owner whitewashed approximately 49 murals in a prominent outdoor art venue without giving the artists notice. A federal court found that the works had achieved recognized stature and awarded $6.75 million in damages. The building owner's argument that he owned the building did not override the artists' VARA rights.

The practical implication: any mural that receives media coverage, becomes a social media destination, or is recognized by the local arts community could potentially qualify for recognized stature — and the recognition often develops after the mural is painted, not before.

## What VARA does not cover

VARA's scope is significant but bounded.

**Works made for hire.** If the muralist is an employee (not an independent contractor), VARA does not apply to that work. For independent contractor muralists — which describes virtually all commercial mural commissions — VARA applies.

**Natural deterioration.** VARA does not prevent a mural from fading, weathering, or degrading naturally over time. The law is concerned with intentional modification or destruction, not the passage of time.

**Works that do not achieve recognized stature.** Many murals will never achieve the community recognition that triggers VARA's strongest protections. A generic decorative painting in a private back hallway is unlikely to qualify.

**Waived rights.** This is the key mechanism. VARA rights cannot be transferred, but they can be waived in writing. A muralist who signs a written waiver gives the property owner significantly more flexibility for modification or removal. The waiver must be signed before the work begins.

## How mural removal works under VARA

The practical scenarios depend on the circumstances.

**If the mural can be removed without destruction:** If the mural is painted on removable panels, canvas, or another separable medium, VARA requires the property owner to give the artist 90 days' written notice before removal. During that window, the artist has the right to remove the work at their own expense. After 90 days, the property owner may remove it.

**If the mural is painted directly on the wall and cannot be removed without destruction:** This is the situation most property owners are actually in. If the mural has achieved recognized stature, destroying it without the artist's permission may be a VARA violation — regardless of whether the property owner has any other agreement with the artist.

**If the artist has signed a written waiver:** A properly executed written waiver, signed before the work begins, allows the property owner to modify or remove the mural without triggering VARA liability. This is the cleanest resolution and the professional standard for commercial mural commissions.

## Why contracts solve most VARA problems

The anxiety around VARA usually comes from not addressing it before the mural is painted. Addressed upfront in the contract, VARA is a standard provision, not a dispute waiting to happen.

A professional mural contract should specify whether the artist waives their VARA rights, and if so, under what conditions. It should also address: what notice the property owner must give before modification or removal, what happens if the artist and property owner disagree about the mural's condition or appearance, and what the process is if the building is sold.

When both parties understand and agree to these terms before the painting begins, VARA disputes rarely arise. The contract does not need to be adversarial — it is simply a record of what everyone agreed to.
